Kiket models real-world processes as **finite state machines**. Issues move through **states**; **transitions** are the arrows between them. Each state can demand approvals, documents, checklists, or SLA timers; the **engine** enforces them. Every change the engine makes lands in the **audit trail**.

## Workflow

A workflow defines *what your process actually is*. It has:

- **States** — named stages an issue can be in (`intake`, `review`, `approved`, `rejected`).
- **Transitions** — directed edges between states, with optional conditions, approvers, and events.
- **Metadata** — SLAs, required documents, checklists, labels — attached per state or per transition.

Workflows live as YAML files in your workspace repo under `.kiket/workflows/`. One file = one workflow.

## State

Every issue is in exactly one state at a time. States have three *categories*:

- **Initial** — where new issues land (usually `intake` or `backlog`).
- **Active** — where work happens; most states fall here.
- **Final** — terminal states; an issue cannot leave a final state (`done`, `rejected`).

A state can declare:

- `sla` — warning and breach thresholds; the engine auto-paged on-call when crossed.
- `required_documents` — transition blockers until the documents are attached.
- `approval` — who must sign off, and in what order.
- `on_enter` / `on_exit` — automations that fire on state change (notifications, webhooks, AI checks).

## Transition

Transitions carry an issue from one state to another. They can be:

- **User-driven** — someone clicks a button on the issue page.
- **Automated** — triggered by a webhook, a schedule, or an AI agent.
- **Conditional** — evaluated against the issue payload (`priority == high && assignee != null`).

Every transition is recorded with actor, timestamp, before/after state, and a hash of the full change.

## Issue

Issues are the *things* flowing through your workflow — contracts, tickets, incidents, change requests. Each has:

- A **workflow key** pinning it to one workflow.
- A **state**, always one of the workflow's declared states.
- **Priority**, **assignee**, **labels**, **due date**, and whatever custom fields the issue type defines.
- **Comments**, **attachments**, **time entries**, **linked issues**.
- An **SLA status** computed from the current state's rules.

Issues are the only thing that lives in the database, not in your git repo. The config (what workflows exist, what states they have) is git-backed; the *runtime data* is a PostgreSQL row.

## SLA

Service-level agreements are per-state timers. Declare them in YAML:

```yaml
states:
  legal_review:
    sla:
      warning: 48h
      breach: 5d
```

The engine emits `sla_warning` and `sla_breach` events when thresholds cross. Notifications, auto-escalations, and dashboard widgets all key off those events.

## Approval

Some transitions require human sign-off. Approval chains can be sequential, parallel, or conditional:

```yaml
transitions:
  - from: legal_review
    to: approval
    approval:
      chain: [counsel, cfo]   # sequential
```

Sequential chains require each approver in order. Parallel chains need all approvers to sign off, order-independent. Conditional chains add a predicate: `if total_value > 100000 then cfo`.

## Audit trail

Every significant action — transitions, approvals, AI suggestions, comments on approvals — is recorded as an immutable event. Events are hashed individually, batched periodically into a Merkle tree, and the root is submitted to the Polygon blockchain.

An auditor can verify your history without talking to us or touching our database. See **[Compliance & audit](/docs/compliance)** for the full details.

## Project, organization, workspace

Three concepts that often blur:

- **Organization** — your company account; billing lives here.
- **Project** — one distinct piece of work (e.g. "Legal" or "Customer Support"); has its own workflows, boards, and members.
- **Workspace** — the git repository that backs a project's configuration. One project = one workspace.

## File-backed config

When you edit a workflow in the visual canvas, the app writes a commit to your workspace repo. When you edit YAML in your IDE and push, the app picks up the change via webhook and re-syncs. Canvas and YAML are two surfaces over the same file.

The fastest way to understand Kiket: create a project, open your workspace repo in your file browser, and watch the `.kiket/` directory change as you use the app.
</Callout>
